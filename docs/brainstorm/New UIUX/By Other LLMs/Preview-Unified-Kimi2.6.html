Why ship

The only direction that is Thai-first at the DNA level — not a universal aesthetic adapted to Thai, but a direction that starts from Thai craft tradition and builds outward. The stamp/chop system makes insurance feel officially verified and recipes feel quality-certified using the same interaction. Component that bridges insurance + meat

The official stamp (ตราประทับ). Insurance articles get a square stamp (authority), cooking articles get a round stamp (tradition), urgent pieces get an oval (movement). Articles without stamps are marked "draft." Same trust-signal system, two shapes.

Why ship

Concrete doesn't lie — it's "เนื้อๆ" rendered as material. The golden light is Thai warmth without a single temple silhouette. Insurance analysis lives in the sharp morning shadows (precision); cooking content lives in the warm zones (generosity). The 38° shadow rule (Bangkok's latitude) is a single CSS rule that replaces every border and box-shadow.

Component that bridges insurance + meat

The shadow line at 38° — every card casts a sharp single-direction shadow with hard edge (no blur). Shadow color is always warm limestone (#D4CFC5). This single rule replaces every box-shadow, border, and divider on the site. Insurance and recipe cards share the same light source.

Trade-off

The "sun-path scroll" gradient overlay (cool morning → golden hour) requires Intersection Observer or scroll-linked CSS custom properties. Safari scroll-linked custom properties can be janky. Also: concrete aesthetic is inherently cool and may feel "unfinished" to readers expecting warmth from a meat brand.

Why ship

Grading is the conceptual bridge — meat gets graded (Prime, Choice, Select), insurance gets graded (A+, B-). The stamp motif unifies both content domains under one visual judgment system. The user reads "เนื้อๆ" and sees certification, not decoration.

Component that bridges insurance + meat

The grade stamp — a circular or elliptical stamp element (2px border, #1B4D3E, slightly rotated -2°) appears on every content piece. Insurance articles get grade stamps like "A+ ผลประกัน" or "B- ความคุ้มค่า"; recipes get "Medium-Rare ✓" or "Well-Done ✗" doneness ratings. The stamp IS the content's verdict.

Trade-off

Every article needs a grade value assigned by the editor — adds editorial overhead. The stamp system can feel overly judgmental if the tone isn't carefully calibrated. Kanit at display sizes is strong but may feel too geometric/cold for the meat half of the brand.

Component that bridges insurance + meat

The single mark — every page has exactly one deliberate mark: a thin (1px) horizontal line in #A0845C that appears once and once only. No doubling, no decoration. It's the hardest to execute well. A mediocre Hearth Cut implementation looks like a broken dark theme. The other directions forgive more; this one offers nowhere to hide. Also: void black + minimal content density = very few articles visible per scroll. Demands exceptionally strong headlines.

Why ship

Temperature is the universal language connecting insurance risk assessment (danger levels) with cooking (doneness levels). The heat-map isn't a metaphor — it's a shared data visualization language that makes both content types legible at a glance. This is "เนื้อๆ" as information architecture.

Component that bridges insurance + meat

The temperature bar — a thin (4px tall) horizontal gradient bar that sits at the top of every content card. Insurance content: gradient runs from #3B7DD8 (safe/blue) through #F5A623 (caution/amber) to #E8482F (critical/red). Recipe content: same gradient mapped to doneness (rare/blue → medium/amber → well-done/red). The bar IS the content preview.

Trade-off

Requires editorial discipline — every article gets a temperature value. Color must be tested for color-blind accessibility (blue → amber → red compresses for deuteranopia). Higher upfront design system cost than simpler directions. The gradient can feel gimmicky if overused.
